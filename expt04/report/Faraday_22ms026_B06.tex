\section{Error Analysis}
\subsection{Random Error}
From the formula of the Verdet constant, we get:
\begin{align}
  \frac{\Delta V}{V}=  \frac{\Delta \theta}{\theta}  + \frac{\Delta B}{B} = \frac{\Delta \theta}{\theta}  + \frac{\Delta I}{I} 
\end{align}
The least count of the current is $0.1\ \text{A}$, while that of the angle measurement in the polariser is $0.1\degree \equiv 6'$. We take the first data point $I= 0.25 \ \text{A}$ and $\theta = 30'$ and $V = 0.02286$ we get 
\begin{align*}
  \Delta V = 0.02286\qty(\frac{6}{30}+ \frac{0.001}{0.25}) = 0.0055
\end{align*}
Hence, we can write the Verdet constant as $V = (0.02571 \pm 0.0055)\  \text{min/Oe/cm}$. We can also calculate the accuracy of the experiment on Malu's law. The deviation in the experimentally derived slope from the theoretical value is, $$\text{Error} = \frac{\abs{2-2.032}}{2} \times 100 \ \% \approx 1.6 \% $$
\subsection{Sources of Error}

Sources of error for this experiment can be:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Non-uniform Magnetic Field:} The experiment assumes that the magnetic field is uniform throughout the material. In reality, it is not perfectly uniform, which introduces error in the measurements.

    \item \textbf{Non-monochromatic Light:} If the light is not monochromatic, different frequency components experience different rotations (due to different responses of RCP and LCP modes). Hence, the incident light should ideally be monochromatic.

    \item \textbf{Thermal Fluctuation:} The Verdet constant and material properties can be temperature dependent. Thermal fluctuations can therefore lead to experimental errors.

    \item \textbf{Alignment Issue:} If the laser is not properly aligned to pass horizontally through the medium, some intensity may be lost. This can lead to incorrect readings in the photodetector.

    \item \textbf{Imperfect Polariser:} If the polarisers are not ideal, the light may not be perfectly polarised. This introduces noise and affects the accuracy of angle measurements.
\end{enumerate}
\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
In this experiment, we investigated the Faraday effect in flint glass and verified Malus' law through intensity measurements. It was observed that the angle of rotation of the plane of polarisation increases linearly with the applied magnetic field, as expected theoretically. The Verdet constant was experimentally determined to be
\[
V = (0.0257 \pm 0.0055) \ \text{min/Oe/cm},
\]
which is consistent across the measured range of currents.\\[0.2cm]
In the second part, the variation of transmitted intensity with current was studied. The log-log analysis showed a power-law dependence with exponent $2.032 \pm 0.008$, confirming the quadratic relationship predicted by Malus' law under small-angle approximation.\\[0.2cm]
Thus, the experiment successfully validates both the linear dependence of Faraday rotation on magnetic field and the quadratic dependence of transmitted intensity on the rotation angle.
